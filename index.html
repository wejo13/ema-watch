The 4H 200 EMA

Every coin's price is compared to its own 200-period EMA on the 4-hour timeframe. ABOVE means the last 4H close is trading above that line; BELOW means it's trading under it. On charts, the solid amber line is this EMA drawn natively on the 4H view; on the 12H/1D/3D/1W charts, the same 4H EMA value is shown as a dashed violet reference line so you can see where that level sits relative to higher-timeframe structure (it's hidden on the 4H chart itself since it would just duplicate the solid line).

Score: Bad / Good / Excellent / Excellent+

The score combines two things: whether price is above or below the 4H 200EMA, and whether a structural low has been swept recently on the 4H, 12H, or 3D timeframe.

Bad — price is below the 4H 200EMA and no recent sweep was found on any of the three timeframes.
Good — price is below the 4H 200EMA, but a recent sweep was found.
Excellent — price is above the 4H 200EMA, with a recent sweep on exactly one of 4H/12H/3D.
Excellent+ — price is above the 4H 200EMA, with a recent sweep on two or more of those timeframes at the same time — a stronger signal than just one lining up.

"Recent" has a strict, deliberately short window so the score doesn't light up on stale, irrelevant history: max 3 candles ago on 4H, max 1 candle ago on 12H, max 1 candle ago on 3D.

On top of that, distance from the EMA acts as a ceiling on the score, since this system never chases price. If price is more than 6% away from the 4H 200EMA, the score is capped at Bad no matter how clean the sweep looks. Between 2% and 6% away, the score is capped at Good at most. Within 2%, there's no cap and the sweep logic decides the tier freely. This cap only ever pulls a score down — it never raises one.

DIST % FROM 200EMA and the small dot next to it

How far the current price is from the 4H 200EMA, as a percentage. The tiny dot next to the number is a separate, independent indicator of how extended that distance is: dim/grey means tight (within 2%), amber means extended (2-8%), red means very extended (8%+, meaningful chasing risk). This dot uses its own thresholds and is shown regardless of what the score cap above decides.

SWEPT column

Shows which timeframe(s) had a qualifying recent sweep — 4H, 12H, 3D, or a combination like "4H+12H" if more than one lined up at once. The small "Xc ago" text next to it shows how many candles ago the freshest of those sweeps happened, on whichever timeframe it occurred on. A dash means no qualifying sweep was found on any timeframe.

RVOL

Relative volume: the current 4H candle's trading volume compared to the average of that same coin's previous 20 four-hour candles. 1.0x is normal for that coin; higher numbers (shown in amber once above 1.5x) mean unusually high participation on the current candle, which often adds weight to a sweep or breakout rather than it happening on thin, easily-reversed volume. This is a per-coin, per-candle read — not a comparison across different coins, and unrelated to the volume used to decide which 100 coins make this list in the first place.

FUNDING column and open interest dot

Where available (not every coin has a Bybit perpetual contract), the FUNDING column shows that coin's current perpetual futures funding rate. Hovering the small dot next to it also shows the 24-hour open interest change. Negative funding means short positions are paying long positions — a sign of crowded short positioning, which can be a contrarian-bullish tell that pairs naturally with this system's liquidity-sweep logic. The dot is colored green-ish when that contrarian read leans bullish, red-ish when it leans cautionary, and grey when it's roughly neutral or there's no data for that coin. A dash means no perpetual contract was found for that coin on Bybit.

Alerts

The alert panel above the table can check, once per hour while this page stays open in a browser tab, whether any coin that was trading above its 4H 200EMA has come back down and touched it. When that happens, a browser notification fires (if permission was granted) and the event is logged below. The log persists across reloads on this device/browser, but alerts only run while the tab is actually open — they can't notify you if the page or browser is closed.

Chart markers (inside a coin's chart popup)

Green/red candle wicks show whether that candle closed above or below its own open. The horizontal markers mark out old swing lows where stop-loss liquidity tends to cluster: a grey dashed marker is a low that's never been touched (still "resting"), a blue marker means price swept below that low but hasn't closed back above it yet, and a thicker white marker means price swept below it and then closed back above it with a bullish candle — the sweep-and-reclaim pattern this whole system is built around hunting for.

The watchlist itself

This list isn't a fixed 100 coins — it's rebuilt from Bybit's USDT perpetual pairs, ranked by 7-day average trading volume (not just the last 24 hours, which can be skewed by a single spike day), then filtered to only coins with at least $450M in market cap via CoinGecko, with known stablecoins excluded entirely. The goal is to keep the list to genuinely liquid, real assets rather than thin or wash-traded pairs.

Stocks tab — manual refresh only

Stocks use a different data source (Twelve Data) than crypto, since the free crypto-style feeds don't allow browser-based requests for stock data. That free tier is rate-limited (8 requests/minute, 800/day), so unlike crypto, stock data is never fetched automatically on page load or by the main refresh button — it only loads when the "refresh stock data" button on this tab is clicked. A full refresh can take a minute or more if the watchlist has more than 8 symbols, since it fetches in rate-limit-safe batches with a pause between each; the status text shows real progress during that wait. Stock data also only has daily-close granularity (not 4H like crypto), so distance from the 200EMA is a daily-equivalent approximation.
